How Should You Invest as You Get Older?¶

Every dollar you have faces two quiet decisions, over and over, for your whole life:

  1. Spend it now, or save it for later?
  2. If you save it, does it go somewhere safe, or into the stock market?

Most of us answer by gut feel. But you can also ask a computer to answer it properly — to work out the smartest possible plan for every single year of a life, from your first paycheck at 20 to your last birthday at 100.

That's what this piece is about. Under the hood sits a well-known financial model — the kind economists have spent decades refining to study how people really save, spend, and invest across a lifetime. We rebuilt a classic version of it and let it play out over 10,000 simulated lives.

Here's the headline it keeps landing on:

When you're young, go heavy on stocks. As you age, ease off.

Not because stocks get riskier — but because you change. Let's see why.

The whole idea, in one picture¶

Look at the middle panel first — it's the punchline.

That line is the share of savings the model puts into stocks at each age. In your early 20s it's pinned at 100%. By your late 50s it has slid down to around 25%. Then, oddly, it drifts back up in retirement.

The left and right panels show the backdrop. Your wealth (the blue line) balloons up to retirement and then draws back down, while your spending (red) stays remarkably smooth the whole way. Nobody wants their lifestyle to lurch around from year to year — and neither does the model.

So why does the "right" stock share start at the ceiling and fall? The answer is the single most useful idea in the whole thing.

Your paycheck is a hidden safe asset¶

Picture a 25-year-old. She has almost nothing in the bank — but she owns something huge that never shows up on a balance sheet: forty more years of paychecks ahead of her.

Those future paychecks behave a lot like a giant, boring bond. They arrive steadily, they don't rise and fall with the stock market's mood, and they're already hers in every way that matters. Economists call this her human capital, and for someone young it dwarfs whatever is in the actual savings account.

Here's the twist that makes everything click:

If most of your total wealth is already sitting in a safe, bond-like form — your future wages — then the small slice you've actually saved can afford to be adventurous. Putting 100% of a tiny savings pot into stocks still leaves your overall mix nicely balanced, because the invisible bond of your career is doing the steadying.

Now fast-forward. At 60, most of those paychecks are behind you. The safe, bond-like part of your wealth has shrunk to almost nothing. So the savings you can see now have to carry the safety themselves — and the model shifts them toward safer assets to make up for it.

That's the glide path in the middle panel. It isn't really about stocks getting scarier with age. It's about your hidden bond — your earning power — quietly running down.

What a lifetime of good decisions looks like¶

Go back to the left panel of that chart. It's a tidy little biography of your finances:

  • Your 20s and 30s: income is climbing, savings are thin. You spend most of what you earn, and what little you invest goes almost entirely into stocks.
  • Your 40s to mid-60s: peak earning years. Wealth (the blue line) climbs steeply as savings compound. This is the hump.
  • Retirement: the paychecks stop, and you start spending down the mountain you built. Wealth slopes back toward the ground.

Through all of it, the red spending line barely wobbles. That's not an accident — it's the most important job the model is doing. It quietly shovels money from your flush years into your lean years so your standard of living stays steady. Save hard when it's raining money; lean on those savings when it isn't.

The more you have, the safer you play¶

Here's a second, subtler pattern — and it holds at every age.

Don't worry that each chart has two colored lines — those are just two versions of the same model landing in the exact same spot (more on why that matters in the trust section below). Follow the shape instead.

The bottom row shows how much of your savings goes into stocks depending on how much cash you're sitting on — the left edge is nearly broke, the right edge is flush. The pattern is striking: the more money you have, the smaller the fraction you put in stocks.

Why? The same hidden-bond logic. When your savings are small next to the paychecks still ahead of you, that safe career income steadies everything, so your little pot can go all-in on stocks. As your savings grow large — larger than the earning power you have left — there's no invisible bond to lean on anymore, so the money itself has to turn conservative.

The real-life takeaway: the right stock allocation isn't one magic number. It depends on how big your savings are relative to the earning power you still have ahead of you.

The retirement plot twist¶

One detail in that first chart looks backwards. After you retire, the stock share stops falling and starts creeping back up. Shouldn't a 75-year-old be the most cautious of all?

It comes down to a race between two things as you spend down your savings in retirement:

  • Your pension and retirement income keep arriving — steady and safe. That hidden bond never fully disappears.
  • Your savings pot shrinks a little every year as you draw on it.

As the pot gets smaller while the steady income stays put, that safe income becomes a bigger share of your total picture again. And just like in your 20s, a big safe cushion means the shrinking savings can afford to take a little more risk. So the glide path gently curls back upward at the end.

(That sharp spike right at age 100 is just the model's final year — the last person standing spends everything left. It's an edge of the arithmetic, not real-world advice.)

Can you actually trust a model like this?¶

Fair question. A model is only as good as the ideas and the arithmetic inside it.

On the ideas: this isn't a back-of-an-envelope guess. It's built on decades of peer-reviewed research into how households really earn, save, and invest over a lifetime — the same family of models used by academics and, increasingly, by the people who design retirement products.

On the arithmetic: the original was written years ago in old academic programming languages. We rebuilt it from scratch in modern code, then checked — year by year — that our version reproduces the original's answers exactly, down to eight decimal places. Think of it as remixing a classic track and proving it's note-for-note identical. If the rebuild had drifted even slightly, we'd have caught it.

So while no model is reality, this one is a faithful, transparent version of a genuinely respected piece of financial science.

What this means for your money¶

You don't need to run a simulation to live your life, but a few of the model's lessons are worth pocketing:

  • Time is your risk buffer. When you have decades of earning ahead, you can ride out the stock market's ups and downs. That's the honest case for younger investors leaning heavily into stocks.
  • Glide down, don't lurch. Easing out of stocks gradually as you age — rather than flipping a switch on retirement day — is exactly what the model recommends. It's also the whole idea behind target-date retirement funds, which do this shift for you automatically.
  • It's about proportion, not a magic number. Your right mix depends on how big your savings are compared with the earning power you still have ahead — not on a one-size-fits-all percentage.
  • Protect your spending, not just your portfolio. The model's deepest instinct is to keep your standard of living smooth. Saving in the good years is what buys you calm in the lean ones.

What the model leaves out¶

Every model trades some realism for clarity, and this one deliberately ignores a few things that matter in real life. Worth knowing before you take it too literally:

  • A home. For most people a house is their biggest asset and one of their biggest financial decisions, with its own costs and rules. This model has no housing in it at all — probably its single biggest simplification.
  • You're not the "average person." It solves for one typical household. Real people differ in how much risk they can stomach, how patient they are, and how secure their jobs are — and those differences change the answer.
  • Calm, characterful markets. It assumes stock returns are unpredictable but steady in character — no booms, busts, or long stretches where the market is obviously cheap or expensive.

None of this sinks the big lesson — start bold, glide toward safety, and protect your spending. But it's exactly why a model is a compass, not a map.
